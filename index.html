Worlds Apart

A Comparative Analysis of Neoliberalism & Fascism

The Great Divide: Ideological Opposites

On the surface, these ideologies are antithetical. One champions the free market and individual liberty, while the other exalts the totalitarian state and national unity above all else.

Role of the State

Neoliberalism

Aims for a minimal, "puny" state whose primary role is to protect property rights and enforce contracts, ensuring the smooth operation of the free market.

Fascism

The state is totalitarian and all-encompassing. All aspects of society and the economy are subordinated to its will as the central, organizing force of the nation.

Economic Policy

Neoliberalism

Favors deregulation, privatization, and global free trade. It is anti-union and seeks to reduce the welfare state to maximize economic efficiency and competition.

Fascism

The state directs the economy through corporatism and protectionism. It aims for national self-sufficiency (autarky), subordinating private enterprise to national goals.

Conception of the Individual

Neoliberalism

The individual is a rational, self-interested actor (*homo economicus*) competing in the market. Individual liberty and consumer choice are core values.

Fascism

The individual is subordinate to the state and the nation. The collective will and glory of the nation or race take precedence over individual rights and freedoms.

Ideological Roots

Neoliberalism

Emerged from classical liberalism as a reaction against collectivism, including both fascism and communism, championing market-based solutions.

Fascism

A radical, ultra-nationalist ideology that emerges in direct opposition to both liberal democracy and communism, seeking a "Third Way" via authoritarianism.

The Unlikely Connection: A Pathway to Power?

Despite their differences, modern analysis, influenced by Karl Polanyi's work, suggests that neoliberal policies can inadvertently create the social and economic conditions that are fertile ground for the rise of authoritarianism.

1

Neoliberal Policies Implemented

Widespread deregulation, privatization, and reduction of social safety nets are enacted to "free" the market.

2

Economic Inequality & Alienation

Wealth concentrates, job precarity rises, and large segments of the population feel left behind and disconnected from the economic system.

3

Erosion of Democratic Institutions

Trust in government, unions, and civic organizations declines. Politics feels distant and unresponsive to the needs of ordinary people.

4

A "Political Void" Emerges

Mainstream parties fail to offer compelling alternatives, creating an opening for radical ideologies to gain traction.

5

Rise of Populism & Scapegoating

Authoritarian leaders exploit frustrations by blaming marginalized groups (immigrants, minorities) for the nation's problems.

6

The Rise of "Zombie Fascism"

An authoritarian movement promising order and national rebirth gains power, often with the tacit support of elites fearing a socialist backlash.

Core Tenets of Neoliberal Policy

Neoliberalism is a multifaceted program prioritizing market mechanisms. Key policies include deregulation to remove state controls, privatization of public assets, weakening unions to increase labor flexibility, and promoting global free trade.

Key Societal Effects

The long-term application of these policies has been linked to significant societal shifts, including a measurable rise in wealth inequality, a sharp decline in public trust in key democratic institutions, and a growing sense of political alienation among citizens.

Alternative Political & Economic Models

Beyond the spectrum of neoliberalism and fascism, other political and economic systems offer different balances between the state, the market, and civil society. These models provide a valuable comparative context.

The Giedroyc Doctrine

A Regional Foreign Policy

The Giedroyc Doctrine, developed by Jerzy Giedroyc, is a foundational Polish foreign policy concept. It advocates for reconciliation and close cooperation with Lithuania, Belarus, and Ukraine. The primary goal is to weaken Russian influence in the region and secure Poland's own sovereignty through strong regional partnerships, offering a contrast to purely market-driven international relations.

Gaullism and Rhenish Capitalism

**Gaullism** (French) is characterized by a strong, centralized state that directs economic and foreign policy to ensure national independence and grandeur. It often involves protectionist measures and skepticism towards international organizations that might compromise national sovereignty. It stands in direct contrast to the minimal state of neoliberalism.

**Rhenish Capitalism** (German) is a "social market economy." It emphasizes a robust welfare state, codetermination (worker participation in management), and a long-term, cooperative relationship between businesses, banks, and the state. It is a more regulated, socially-oriented form of capitalism that aims to balance economic growth with social cohesion.

A Warning From History

While not the same, the pursuit of a pure, self-regulating market can destabilize the social fabric that underpins democracy. By creating economic instability and eroding public trust, it can open the door for authoritarian movements that promise order at the cost of freedom.
